Task 1

The visualization shows the new covid-19 cases of 7-day average from Jan. 2020 (the start of pandemic) to Jan. 2022 (rise of Omicron variant). The curve indicating the case numbers is continuous and forms a spiral, so that different years share same month axes. The curve is mirrored to form enclosed colored area so that readers can easily tell when there are more cases and when there are fewer.

The number of cases is nearly invisible (in terms of visualization) at the start of 2020, nearly rises, and reaches a significant peak in the winter crossing 2020 and 2021. It drops down slowly to very small in June 2021 but rises up again through summer 2021. After, the number sees a bit drop and end up at a dramatical rise at the start of 2022.

On a larger aspect, the graph shows three waves of epidemic rises, revealing seasonal pattern, among which the Omicron variant aligns with the first wave in season but is more contagious.

Sketch 3

For the last attempt, I was wondering if # new confirmed cases / # new tested cases would reflect the severity of epidemic more, since pure number of confirmed cases is affected by how many people got tested.

I did notice one issue at the very beginning: there are a lot of days when confirmed cases are more than tested case. I thought that might be a delay in processing test result, so I calculated 7-day average for both columns.

However, the graph turned out to be still quite dramatic, seeing entries more than 500% at the beginning of 2020, and the number goes over 1000% from mid-2021.

After more searches, I realized two more issues with this method: 1) Not a lot of people were tested at the beginning of the pandemic, thus 1 positive cases out of 2 tested cases will be equally severe as 100 positive cases out of 200 cases, which might not be true; 2) On March 2021, the covid tracking project ceased to collect data, which might be the reason why number of tested cases suddenly dropped from millions to thousands on Mar 8, 2021. Also, at-home rapid test kits were available at the later stage of covid. Both resulting in # new test cases much smaller than # new tested cases, making percentage meaningless.
